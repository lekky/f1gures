DRS — the **Drag Reduction System** — is the overtaking aid you will hear
commentators mention every lap. It is a clever, tightly regulated way of giving a
following car a temporary speed boost on the straights.

## What it actually does

A flap in the **rear wing** hinges open when DRS is active. Opening it reduces
aerodynamic drag, letting the car reach a higher top speed on the straight. When
the driver brakes for the next corner, the flap snaps shut and full downforce
returns. It is purely a straight-line tool — there is no cornering benefit.

## The one-second rule

In a race, a driver can only use DRS if they are within **one second** of the car
ahead at a fixed **detection point** on the track. If they are inside that gap,
the system arms, and the driver can then open the wing once they reach the
following **activation zone** — a designated stretch of straight, marked by a DRS
line, where overtaking is most likely. Most circuits have one to three of these
zones. Crucially, DRS helps you attack but offers no defence: the car in front
cannot use it unless it too is within a second of someone.

## When it is free, and when it is off

In **practice and qualifying** there is no one-second requirement — drivers can
use DRS freely in the activation zones to chase a faster lap. In the race it is
only enabled a couple of laps after the start, once the field has settled.

DRS is **disabled** whenever conditions are unsafe: under yellow flags, in wet
conditions, and during [safety car](/guide/safety-cars/) periods. Race control
re-enables it once the track is clear and dry again. Because it depends on staying
within a second of a rival, DRS is closely tied to the slipstream and dirty-air
effects covered in [overtaking](/guide/overtaking/).
