A pit stop looks like chaos but is one of the most rehearsed routines in sport.
Get it right and a driver loses barely 20 seconds of track time; get it wrong and
a race can unravel in the pit lane.

## The stop itself

A crew of around 20 people swarms the car. Four pairs of mechanics handle the
wheels, each running a wheel gun that loosens, removes and refits a tyre in a
blur; others steady the car or work the front-jack and rear-jack. The wheel
change itself takes roughly **two to two-and-a-half seconds**, with the total
time loss mostly coming from the slow drive down the speed-limited pit lane. The
"stop" you hear timed on the broadcast is just the stationary part.

## The two-compound rule

In a dry race, every driver **must** use at least two different dry
[tyre](/guide/tyres/) compounds. That single rule is why nobody can simply bolt
on one set and drive to the flag — at least one pit stop is effectively
mandatory. (In a wet race declared by the stewards, that requirement is waived.)

## The pit window and strategy

The **pit window** is the range of laps in which a stop makes sense — late enough
that the new tyres last to the end, early enough that the old ones haven't fallen
off a cliff. Teams plan one-stop or two-stop races around it, balancing fresh-tyre
pace against time lost in the lane.

The timing of a stop relative to a rival is where strategy gets interesting. Pit
**before** an opponent and use fresh-tyre pace to leapfrog them — the *undercut*.
Stay out **longer** and rely on clear air to build a gap first — the *overcut*.
Both are explained in full, with a worked example, in the
[overtaking](/guide/overtaking/) guide.
