Tyres are the single biggest variable in a race. Every team buys the same rubber
from **Pirelli**, so how a driver manages it often matters more than raw car
speed. Half of any race broadcast is really a conversation about tyres.

## The dry compounds

Pirelli makes a range of dry "slick" tyres, from the hardest to the softest. The
range runs from **C0/C1** at the hard end to **C6** at the soft end, and Pirelli
nominates three of them for each weekend. Those three are then labelled simply as
**hard** (white sidewall), **medium** (yellow) and **soft** (red), so the colours
you see are relative to the weekend, not absolute.

The trade-off is always the same: softer tyres grip harder and go faster but wear
out sooner; harder tyres are slower but last longer. Choosing which to run, and
when, is the heart of strategy.

## Wet-weather tyres

When it rains there are two grooved options. The **intermediate** (green sidewall)
clears a damp or lightly wet track. The **full wet** (blue sidewall) is for
standing water and pumps huge amounts of spray. Drivers switch between these and
slicks as conditions change, and getting that crossover lap right can win or lose
a race.

## The two-compound rule and the cliff

In a dry race, drivers **must** use at least two different dry compounds, which
guarantees at least one stop. As a tyre wears, lap times slowly **degrade**; push
it too far and performance falls off a sudden **cliff** — a steep, non-linear drop
where grip vanishes and lap times balloon. Hearing a driver report they are "off
the cliff" usually means a pit stop is imminent. Managing degradation to stay just
short of that edge is the skill that separates a clean strategy from a ruined one
(see [pit stops & strategy](/guide/pit-stops-and-strategy/)).
