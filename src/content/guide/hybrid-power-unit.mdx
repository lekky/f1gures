An F1 engine is not just an engine. It is a **power unit (PU)**, a hybrid that
combines a petrol engine with an electric system. You do not need the engineering
detail to follow a race, but a basic grasp explains a lot of the radio chatter.

## Two sources of power

The PU pairs a small **1.6-litre V6 turbo** petrol engine with an electric
system, the **ERS** (Energy Recovery System). The petrol engine provides the bulk
of the grunt; the electric motor adds a hefty boost on top. Together they produce
around 1,000 horsepower. For the **2026** rules the balance shifts dramatically:
the electric side grows to roughly half of total power, an even split with the
combustion engine, all running on fully sustainable fuel.

## Harvesting and deployment

The electric system works on a simple cycle. Under braking and on the straights,
the car **harvests** energy and stores it in a battery. The driver then
**deploys** that stored energy for an extra surge of power, typically out of
corners and down straights, where it counts most.

The catch is that the battery is finite. Deploy the full electric boost
everywhere and you will run out before the lap is done; you hear drivers told
they are "out of battery" or managing deployment. From 2026 this energy
management becomes the single biggest performance lever, which is why teams obsess
over it.
