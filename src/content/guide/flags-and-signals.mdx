Marshals around the track communicate with drivers using a set of coloured
flags, increasingly mirrored by LED light panels and dashboard messages. Each
colour carries a specific instruction, and knowing them makes the live timing far
easier to read.

## Hazard flags

A **yellow flag** means danger ahead: a car off the track, debris, a slow car.
Drivers must slow down and cannot overtake in that sector. A **double-waved
yellow** is more serious: be ready to stop, because the track may be blocked.

A **red flag** stops the session entirely. Cars return to the pit lane and the
clock pauses. During a race a red flag has a strategic twist: teams are allowed
to change tyres for free while the cars are stationary, which can hand a lucky
driver a cheap pit stop (see [safety cars](/guide/safety-cars/)).

A **green flag** signals the hazard is cleared and racing resumes at full speed.

## Blue, white and the warning flags

A **blue flag** is shown to a slower car about to be lapped by the leaders; it
must let the faster car through promptly or risk a penalty. A **white flag**
warns of a slow-moving vehicle on track, such as a recovery truck or a car
limping back to the pits.

The **black-and-orange flag** (with a black circle) tells a specific driver they
have car damage and must pit to fix it. A **black flag** is the most severe: it
disqualifies a driver, ordering them back to the pits for good, and is reserved
for serious rule breaches (see [penalties](/guide/penalties/)).

## The chequered flag

The **chequered flag** marks the end of the session. In a race it falls as the
leader crosses the line to complete the final lap; every other car takes the
flag on the lap they are on when the leader finishes.
