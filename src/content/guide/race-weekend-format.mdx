A Formula 1 weekend is a three-day build-up to roughly two hours of racing on
Sunday. The schedule is the same almost everywhere, so once you know the shape
of it you can follow any round on the calendar.

## Practice and qualifying

A standard weekend opens with three free-practice sessions: **FP1** and **FP2**
on Friday, **FP3** on Saturday morning. These are for the teams: they run
different fuel loads and tyre compounds to learn how the car behaves and lock
in a setup. Lap times in practice rarely mean much on their own.

Saturday afternoon is **qualifying**, which sets the starting grid. It runs as a
knockout over three segments. In **Q1**, all 20 cars run and the slowest five are
eliminated; they fill positions 16 to 20. The remaining 15 go to **Q2**, where
the slowest five again drop out into positions 11 to 15. The final 10 contest
**Q3** to decide the front of the grid, with the fastest lap taking pole position.

## Sprint weekends

Six rounds a season swap the format for a **sprint weekend**. There is only one
practice session, FP1, followed by a separate **Sprint Qualifying (SQ)** that
sets the grid for the **Sprint**, a short race of around 100 km with no
mandatory pit stop. After the Sprint, normal qualifying runs to set the grid for
Sunday's Grand Prix as usual. The order is FP1 → SQ → Sprint → Qualifying → Race,
which packs two competitive grids and two results into one weekend.

## The Grand Prix

The main event is Sunday's race, run over a distance of about 305 km (the number
of laps varies by circuit length) or a two-hour time limit, whichever comes
first. Drivers score championship [points](/guide/points-system/) based on where
they finish, and most races require running at least two different
[tyre](/guide/tyres/) compounds, which is what makes pit strategy such a big part
of the show.
