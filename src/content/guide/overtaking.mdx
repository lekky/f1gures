Overtaking in modern F1 is rarely a simple case of one car being faster. It is a
mix of aerodynamics, tyre condition and timing, and a lot of it happens in the
pit lane rather than on track.

## Slipstream and dirty air

A car punches a hole in the air, leaving a low-drag wake behind it. A following
driver who tucks into that **slipstream** (or "tow") gains speed on the straight
and can slingshot past. The catch is **dirty air**: the turbulent wake also robs a
following car of downforce in corners, making it slide and overheat its
[tyres](/guide/tyres/). That is why a car can be glued to a rival on the straights
yet unable to follow closely through the bends.

When several cars were bunched (in pre-2026 races) and each had [DRS](/guide/drs/)
on the car ahead, the advantage cancelled out; a **DRS train** formed and nobody
could break clear.

## Moves into a corner

The classic pass is the **dive-bomb** (or "lunge"): braking later than the car
ahead to grab the inside line into a corner. Defend against it well and you set up
the **switchback**: the defender takes a tight, slow line, lets the attacker run
wide, then cuts back underneath with more speed onto the next straight.

## The undercut and overcut

Most overtakes actually come through strategy. In the **undercut**, you pit
*before* a rival; your fresh tyres are several seconds a lap quicker, so by the
time they stop a lap or two later, you have leapfrogged them.

A worked example: you trail a rival by 1.5 seconds. You pit; your new tyres are
worth 1.2 seconds a lap. After two laps you have clawed back 2.4 seconds, more
than the gap, so when your rival finally pits, they emerge behind you.

The **overcut** is the reverse: you stay out on older tyres while the rival pits
into traffic or onto cold rubber, banking fast laps in clear air, then pit later
and rejoin ahead. Which one works depends on tyre wear, track position and how
much "dirty air" the cars are fighting.
