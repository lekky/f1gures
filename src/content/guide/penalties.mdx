When a driver breaks the rules (causing a collision, gaining an advantage off
track, or making an unsafe release in the pits), the **stewards** decide on a penalty. There
is a graded menu of them, and knowing the difference tells you how badly a result
is about to change.

## Time penalties

The most common are **5-second** and **10-second** time penalties. The driver
serves them at their next pit stop, sitting stationary for the required time
before the crew may touch the car, or, if they do not stop again, the time is
simply added to their total at the end of the race. A small penalty can drop a
driver several positions if rivals are close behind.

## Drive-through and stop-go

More serious is the **drive-through**: the driver must enter the pit lane, drive
through it at the speed limit and rejoin without stopping, losing around 20
seconds. Harsher still is the **10-second stop-go**, where the car must stop in
its box for ten full seconds with no work allowed, then rejoin. Both cost far more
than a time penalty.

## Grid drops and licence points

Some penalties carry over to the next race as a **grid-place drop**, common for
taking too many engine components over a season, or for a driving offence judged
too late in a race to serve. A driver might start, say, five places lower than
they qualified.

Separately, the FIA runs a **super-licence penalty-points** system. Each serious
offence adds points to a driver's licence, and accumulating **12 points within 12
months** triggers an automatic **one-race ban**. Points expire on a rolling
12-month basis, so a clean run gradually wipes the slate. The most severe
in-race sanction of all is **disqualification**, signalled by the black flag
covered in [flags & signals](/guide/flags-and-signals/).
