There are 71 laps of the [Red Bull Ring](/circuits/red_bull_ring/) on Sunday,
and the team whose name is painted on the grandstands will spend most of them
looking at the wrong end of the timing screen. Seven rounds into 2026, Red
Bull are fourth in the constructors', winless, and watching <DriverChip ref="max_verstappen" />
sit seventh in a drivers' table led by a teenager in a Mercedes. The home
race was supposed to be the highlight of their summer. It now looks more like
an audit.

Spielberg is a strange little circuit - barely over a minute a lap, ten
corners, three uphill straights that each end in a heavy braking zone. It
flatters cars that stop well and put power down out of slow corners, and it
exposes the ones that don't. On current form, that is not good news for the
hosts.

The numbers tell two stories. Mercedes have turned a fast car into a runaway -
72 points clear of Ferrari - while the team behind them is the one quietly
building momentum. <DriverChip ref="hamilton" /> won at Barcelona from second
on the grid, his first victory in red, and has hauled himself up to second in
the championship. Ferrari leave Spain looking like the only team with the pace
to ask Mercedes a question over the rest of the year.

## What Spielberg asks of the cars

The Red Bull Ring is a traction-and-braking circuit dressed up as a power
track. **Braking stability:** turns 1, 3 and 4 are all big stops at the top
of a climb, and a car that locks or squirms under braking loses the lap there.
**Traction out of slow corners:** the exits of 3, 4 and 6 feed straight onto
the next straight, so how cleanly the car deploys matters more than top speed.
**Kerb tolerance:** the exit kerbs are punishing, and track limits at turns 9
and 10 have decided results here before.

<PullQuote>“The lap is so short that one tenth is three places. There is nowhere to hide a weak corner.”</PullQuote>

The 2026 rules add a twist. Three DRS zones and the active-aero system make
this one of the easiest tracks to follow and pass on - good for the racing,
awkward for anyone hoping to defend track position. Qualifying matters, but
unlike Imola or Monaco, a fast car starting out of position can still carve
back through the field. That suits Ferrari, and it suits a Mercedes that has
qualified on the front row at all but one round.
