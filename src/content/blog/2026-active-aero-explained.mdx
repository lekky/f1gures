The [2026 power-unit reset](/blog/2026-power-unit-regulations-explained/) got
most of the pre-season attention - half-electric power, no MGU-H, sustainable
fuel. But the change drivers talk about most isn't under the engine cover. It's
the wings. For the first time, the bodywork that makes downforce moves on
purpose, on every lap, on both axles of the car. DRS is gone. This is what took
its place.

## Why the wings had to move

The 2026 power unit leans on electrical energy for roughly half its output, and
a battery only holds so much. Deploy 350 kW down every straight and you run dry
before the lap is done. The fix the regulations chose was aerodynamic: let the
car shed drag on the straights so the same battery carries the car further and
faster. A wing that stays in its high-downforce shape all lap is, on a straight,
just a brake. So the wings were told to get out of the way.

## Two modes: Z and X

Both the front and rear wings carry movable elements, and they work as a pair
between two configured states:

- **Z-mode** - high downforce. The wings sit in their full-grip shape for
  braking and cornering.
- **X-mode** - low drag. The elements flatten out on the straights, cutting drag
  so the car carries more speed for the same energy.

The driver selects X-mode on the straight; the car automatically snaps back to
Z-mode the instant the brakes are touched. The front wing moving in concert with
the rear is the genuinely new part - it keeps the aerodynamic balance stable as
the car switches states, which a rear-only system (like DRS) never had to manage.

<PullQuote>“DRS gave you one flap and one zone. Now the whole car breathes down every straight - and so does the car ahead of you.”</PullQuote>

## The crucial difference from DRS

DRS was a *combat* tool. You only got to open the flap if you were within one
second of the car ahead, in a designated zone. It was an overtaking aid handed
to the chaser.

Active aero is not that. **Every car runs X-mode on every straight, all race
long, whether or not anyone is ahead of it.** The low-drag mode is a lap-time
tool, not a passing tool. That removes the artificial "DRS train" dynamic - but
it also removes the guaranteed closing speed a following car used to get. If
overtaking were left there, 2026 would have made passing *harder*.

## So how do you overtake now?

The passing aid moved from the wings to the battery. In place of DRS, a
following car gets a **manual override** - a short burst of extra electrical
deployment, triggered when you're close enough behind. It hands the chaser
*power*, not grip: a few hundred extra kilowatts down the straight rather than a
slug of free downforce reduction.

The live debate of the season is whether that trade produces better racing.
Power-based overtaking rewards a car that can put energy down cleanly out of the
preceding corner; aero-based overtaking rewarded raw straight-line slip. Early
returns are mixed - the racing looks more natural where the battery has charge
to spend, and noticeably flatter where energy-poor cars arrive at the straight
with nothing left in the override.

## Why some weekends barely show it

Active aero's value scales with how much straight a circuit has. On a layout
full of long full-throttle runs, X-mode is worth a fortune and the override is
decisive. On a tight, low-speed track it barely registers - there's no straight
long enough to matter, and qualifying position becomes everything.

That's why the 2026 storyline has swung circuit to circuit. At low-speed venues
like [Monaco](/races/2026/6/) the system all but disappeared and track position
ruled; at the [Red Bull Ring](/races/2026/8/), with three short straights each
ending in a heavy stop, the cars that deployed cleanly out of the slow corners
carried the day. Same regulations, completely different weekend.

## The hidden cost: driver workload

DRS was one button in one zone. Active aero is a mode the driver manages lap
after lap, woven into the same energy budget that the [power
unit](/blog/2026-power-unit-regulations-explained/) already made the hardest
puzzle on the car. When to hold Z-mode for stability, when to commit to X-mode
and trust the front to follow, when to spend the override and when to bank the
charge for defence - it's a constant, lap-long calculation.

It is, in other words, of a piece with the rest of 2026: less of the performance
handed to the driver by a single button, more of it earned by managing a car
that asks a question on every straight. Whether that makes for better racing than
DRS is the argument that will define how this rule set is remembered. Whether it
makes for *harder* racing isn't in doubt.
