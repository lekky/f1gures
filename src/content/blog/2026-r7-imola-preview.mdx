The 2026 season has been many things — fast, loud, unfamiliar — but it has
not yet been **close**. Four of the first six rounds have gone to the same
car, and the championship table reads like a McLaren internal memo. Imola is
the first real test of whether that's a quirk of the calendar or the shape of
the year.

This is the kind of circuit that has historically scrambled the form book.
Narrow, undulating and starved of overtaking, the Autodromo Enzo e Dino
Ferrari rewards precision over raw aero efficiency — and punishes the smallest
mistake with a wall. For the first time in 2026, qualifying may matter more
than race pace.

## What Imola asks of the cars

Three demands separate Imola from the opening flyaways. **Kerb tolerance:** the
chicanes are aggressive and the cars that can ride them carry the laptime.
**Mechanical grip:** with low average speeds, this is less about downforce and
more about how cleanly the car puts power down out of slow corners. **Track
position:** with one DRS zone and barely a clean overtaking spot, where you
start is very nearly where you finish.

<PullQuote>“At Imola you don’t win on Sunday. You win on Saturday, and survive on Sunday.”</PullQuote>

The 2026 regulations sharpen all of this. The active-aero system that bleeds
drag on the straights is at its least useful on a lap with so few of them, and
the narrower tyres give less margin over the kerbs. Expect the engineers who
nail the mechanical balance on Friday to be the ones smiling on Sunday.
